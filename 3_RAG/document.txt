Generative AI refers to a class of artificial intelligence systems that can produce new content, including text, images, audio, and video, based on patterns learned from large amounts of training data. Unlike traditional AI systems that were built to classify or predict, generative models learn the underlying structure of data well enough to create entirely new examples that resemble the training distribution. The most prominent examples today are large language models such as GPT-4, Claude, and LLaMA, which are trained on hundreds of billions of words scraped from the internet, books, and scientific papers. These models learn to predict the next word in a sequence, and through that simple objective, they develop surprisingly deep representations of language, reasoning, and world knowledge. The scale of compute and data required to train these models is enormous, often costing millions of dollars and requiring thousands of specialised GPUs running for weeks at a time.

The applications of generative AI are expanding rapidly across nearly every industry. In healthcare, language models are being used to summarise patient records, assist with diagnosis, and accelerate drug discovery by generating candidate molecular structures. In software engineering, tools like GitHub Copilot use generative models to autocomplete code, suggest refactors, and even write entire functions from a plain English description. Marketing teams use generative AI to draft copy, personalise emails at scale, and produce social media content in a fraction of the time it would take a human. Legal and financial firms are experimenting with document summarisation and contract analysis to reduce the hours their professionals spend on routine review work. Education platforms are building personalised tutoring systems that adapt explanations based on a student's level and learning style. The common thread across all these use cases is that generative AI acts as a force multiplier, allowing small teams to produce outputs that previously required much larger ones.

Despite its promise, generative AI comes with significant challenges and risks that researchers and policymakers are actively grappling with. Hallucination is one of the most well-known problems, referring to the tendency of language models to generate confident-sounding statements that are factually incorrect. This happens because these models are fundamentally pattern matchers, not truth engines, and they have no reliable mechanism to verify claims against reality. Bias is another serious concern, since models trained on internet data inevitably absorb the prejudices, stereotypes, and skewed representations present in that data. There are also substantial questions around copyright and intellectual property, as generative models are trained on human-created content without always obtaining consent or providing compensation. The environmental cost of training and running these models is significant, with large training runs emitting carbon comparable to multiple transatlantic flights. Finally, the potential for misuse, including generating disinformation, deepfakes, and phishing content at scale, has made AI safety and governance an urgent priority for governments and research labs alike.
